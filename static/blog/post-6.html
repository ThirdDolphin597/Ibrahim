Intro

I started by finalizing the breadboard PCB and fully testing the hall effect sensor. Once that was working reliably, I shifted focus to the final PCB: updating component packages, improving trace reliability, and adding a USB‑C breakout so the board could sit cleanly in the enclosure.

With the PCB design locked in, I dropped it into the draft enclosure model and added a toggle switch and the hall effect sensor model. The goal was to make a box where the PCB and all electronics slide out together on a tray, while keeping the USB‑C connector aligned to a cutout in the case.

The PCB

I refined the PCB traces so no nets are left hanging or unconnected. This cleaned up the routing and made the design ready for fabrication.

Cleaned-up PCB routing.

I also added a complete model of the USB-C breakout board. The breakout model makes it easy to create a matching cutout in the box so the port sits flush and the board can be secured in place.

USB‑C breakout on PCB.

Magnetic Assembly

As mentioned before, the tray attaches to the box with magnets. For this case I used twelve 6mm x 2mm magnets. I designed six holes on the top and six on the bottom to hold everything together firmly while still allowing easy removal. I plan to superglue the magnets into their slots.

Magnet hole layouts for top and bottom halves.

Cross section showing internal layers and magnet placement.

Conclusion

With the 3D model finalized, the next step is to bring everything to life. I plan to 3D print the enclosure and mill the PCB to verify the design works in the real world. Once I have the physical parts, I'll test-fit all the electronics and magnets to make sure everything assembles smoothly. After confirming the fit and function, I'll laser cut the final Microsoft Edge logo out of acrylic to complete the lightbox.
