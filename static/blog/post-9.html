Intro

This week I finished the LED lightbox project and started working on a new camera cage. The lightbox is now complete with the acrylic diffuser installed and the hall effect sensor working perfectly. With the lightbox done, I've moved on to designing and prototyping the camera cage.

Finishing the Lightbox

I wrapped up all the remaining work on the lightbox by laser cutting the acrylic diffuser with the Microsoft Edge logo design. Once the acrylic was cut and installed, I integrated the hall effect sensor and ran multiple tests. Everything worked flawlessly on the first try. The LEDs light up and the hall effect sensor picks up the magnet through the plastic.

Designing the Handle and the Cage

I started the camera cage design process by drawing inspiration from similar products on Smallrig's website. I designed both the handle and cage around a camera model I found online to ensure proper fitment and functionality.

The handle was my first component to design. It features two cold shoes on top specifically designed to mount lights or a camera monitor. My initial plan was to attach the handle to the camera using a cold shoe mount, but I miscalculated the dimensions and the cold shoes ended up too thin and broke during testing. Luckily, I added a screw hole attachment option that connects the handle to the cage instead.

Camera Handle Model.

For the cage itself, I initially designed it to be 3D printed around the camera model. However, I decided to scrap that approach in favor of a more robust solution. The new design calls for the cage to be machined from 1/4 inch aluminum and waterjet cut, with a full threading pass afterward. The connecting holes on the sides will be drilled on a drill press, though I'll likely need to 3D print a holder to keep the pieces upright and positioned correctly during drilling.

The final cage design features 1/4-20 holes spaced 9 mm apart all around the cage. This spacing is intentional—most side handles use screws spaced 18 mm apart, so this spacing gives maximum flexibility for accessory mounting. At the top, there's a 3/8-16 hole to connect the new handle I'll be designing from scratch. The cage itself attaches to the camera using a 1/4-20 screw from below.

Camera Cage Model.

Prototyping the Cage

I 3D printed the cage prototype out of translucent PLA to test tolerances and verify how well it fits the camera. This prototype served as a crucial test to ensure all the dimensions were correct before moving to the metal version. I assembled it using metal screws, manually threading the holes with the screws to create the threads in the plastic. This gave me confidence that the design would work properly once fabricated in aluminum.

Complete overview of the translucent PLA prototype cage.

Side views showing the hole spacing and construction.

The assembled cage.

After assembling the prototype, I brought it home and tested it on the actual camera. I discovered a couple of issues that need to be addressed in the final design. One of the sides where the cage attaches was too thin and needs reinforcement. Additionally, the cage was rubbing against the right side of the camera, so I'll need to extend that section of the cage to provide more clearance. The plastic prototype did flex a bit, but this shouldn't be an issue with the aluminum version, which will be much more rigid. Aside from these two modifications, the rest of the design is solid and ready for production.

The Completed Rig

Here's the completed camera rig with all the accessories mounted. I attached a phone holder and an Amaran Ace 20c light to the cage using 1/4-20 screws. The cage's standardized hole pattern made it easy to mount these accessories without any issues.

I'm currently using the broken handle as a temporary solution, but I plan to redesign it from scratch to be larger, with additional cold shoes and screw hole options for better versatility and durability.

Next Steps

Looking ahead, I have several important tasks to complete. First, I'll make the design changes I identified during testing—reinforcing the thin attachment side and extending the cage where it was rubbing against the camera. Once those modifications are finalized, I'll redesign the handle from scratch to be larger and more robust, then print it out to test. Finally, I'll prepare the cage design for manufacturing and send it out to be waterjet cut from aluminum. After waterjetting, the cage will be threaded and ready for assembly.
